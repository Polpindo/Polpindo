ACADEMIC ESSAY

ASHURA 10 MUHARRAM (عَاشُورَاء): A SACRED PALIMPSEST IN THE MEMORY OF CIVILIZATION

(A Holistic Analysis from Ancient History and Abrahamic Theology to Sufistic Philosophy)

SIGIT N. MASHARY
Saturday, 5 July 2025

To reflect upon the 10th of Muharram (Ashura) is to gaze into the mirror of time: therein lies a parting sea, flowing blood, and a flame of faith that never extinguishes.

— Synthesis from Ibn Kathir (Tafsir) and Ayoub (Redemptive Suffering)

 

The 10th day of Muharram, or Ashura, represents a unique religious and cultural phenomenon, functioning as a palimpsest—an ancient manuscript where new meanings are continuously written over older layers without ever completely erasing them. This essay traces the evolution of Ashura through a multidisciplinary analysis, encompassing a historical review from early civilizations to the modern era, an etymological-linguistic investigation, a theological comparison between Judaism, Sunni Islam, and Shia Islam, and concludes with an interpretation of how this day shapes memory and identity. By tracking its transformation, this essay argues that Ashura is a prime case study of how a ritual can be assimilated, diverged, and recontextualized to serve the theological, political, and social needs of different communities throughout history, and ultimately, become a mirror for God's Self-disclosure (tajalli).

CHAPTER I

Archaic Echoes – The Universal Symbolism of the "Tenth Day"

Long before Ashura was codified within the Abrahamic traditions, the number "ten" held a sacred resonance in various ancient civilizations, often marking a moment of transition, completion, or cosmic reconciliation. These cultures, though separated by geography and time, exhibit a universal pattern in viewing the tenth day as the culmination of a spiritual cycle.

In the ancient Hebrew Calendar, the tenth day of the month of Tishrei was established as Yom Kippur (the Day of Atonement), the climax of the "Ten Days of Repentance" that begin with Rosh Hashanah.1 It is the final day of purification before entering the new year. In ancient Persia, Zoroastrian culture celebrated the transition from darkness to light, where the days leading up to Nowruz (the New Year) held ritualistic significance, with the number ten symbolizing completeness.2 The Ancient Romans dedicated the 10th of January to the festival of Agonalia, a sacrificial ritual aimed at securing divine protection and courage for the year ahead.3

Even on the American continent, the Mayan people viewed their cycles of time with sacred sensitivity. In their calendar, the five-day period of Wayeb' was considered perilous. Days approaching and reaching multiples of ten in larger cycles were often accompanied by fasting and rituals to appease the gods and ensure a safe cosmic transition.4 This cross-cultural pattern suggests that the interpretation of the tenth day as a "critical moment" demanding rituals of purification, sacrifice, or introspection is not coincidental. It is rooted in humanity's archaic intelligence in understanding time not merely as a linear cycle, but as a sacred interweaving of the human world, nature, and the divine. This symbolic foundation became the stage for the historical drama of Ashura.

CHAPTER II

Etymological Traces and the Pre-Islamic Landscape

Before Islam transformed its meaning, Ashura already existed within the cultural and linguistic landscape of Arabia. Etymological analysis reveals its deep roots and complex relationship with the Semitic world and beyond. The Arabic word "Ashura" (عَاشُورَاء) is convincingly derived from the Semitic root for "ten." Its phonetic pattern, fāʿūlāʾ, is often used in Arabic to denote a significant day or event.5

This connection becomes clearer when considering the context of Aramaic, the lingua franca of pre-Islamic Jewish communities. The fast of 10 Tishrei (Yom Kippur) was called Yoma Asura in Aramaic dialects, literally meaning "the tenth day," a phrase whose pronunciation very likely influenced the Arabic term.6 Beyond the Semitic world, some speculative theories suggest a semantic exchange. The term Ashur in Old Persian referred to a "god," while the Sanskrit word Asura in the Rig Veda initially had a positive connotation as a "spiritual being" before evolving to mean "anti-god."7 Although there is no direct evidence, the exchange of cosmological concepts about the struggle between order and chaos via ancient spice trade routes remains an intriguing possibility.

Historically, pre-Islamic Arab tribes, including the Quraysh in Mecca, had already observed Ashura as a day of thanksgiving and purification. A hadith narrated by Aisha confirms that the Quraysh fasted on this day and that Prophet Muhammad himself followed the practice before the Hijra.8 This practice was associated with reverence for the Ka'bah, on which day the ritual of replacing its covering (the kiswah) was performed, marking an annual sacred renewal.9 Thus, pre-Islamic Ashura was not a vacuum but an established ritual, firmly rooted in local tradition, which would later be adopted and imbued with new meaning by Islam.

CHAPTER III

The Meeting Point and the Breaking Point – Ashura in the Abrahamic Tradition

The advent of Islam marked the most fundamental transformative point for Ashura, changing it from a local tradition into a significant marker in the Abrahamic narrative, before a tragedy ultimately fractured it into two irreconcilable meanings.

For Judaism, as discussed, 10 Tishrei is Yom Kippur, a day of communal and personal atonement based on the Torah's command (Leviticus 16). Its focus is on introspection, fasting, and prayer for reconciliation with God. When Prophet Muhammad migrated to Medina, he found the Jewish community fasting on this day to commemorate the salvation of Prophet Moses and the Israelites from Pharaoh's pursuit.

Herein lies the first meeting point. In a brilliant move, Prophet Muhammad adopted this fasting practice, stating, "We have more right to Moses than you do."10 With this, he not only confirmed a tradition he was already familiar with in Mecca but also "Islamized" it by providing a new theological narrative. The Ashura fast was recontextualized as a symbol of gratitude for the victory of Moses, a highly revered prophet in the Qur'an. It was a strategy to claim the same Abrahamic heritage, while later establishing a distinct Islamic identity by recommending a fast on the 9th day (Tasu'a) to "differ from the Jews."11 This is the meaning of Ashura in the Sunni Islamic tradition: a day of voluntary fasting, reflection, and celebration of divine grace.

However, on the 10th of Muharram, 61 AH (680 CE), a breaking point occurred that changed everything. On the plains of Karbala, Husayn ibn Ali, the grandson of Prophet Muhammad, along with his family and loyal followers, was tragically massacred by the army of the Caliph Yazid ibn Muawiyah. For the community that would come to be known as Shia Muslims, this event superseded all previous meanings of Ashura. The day of thanksgiving turned into the darkest day of mourning. Karbala became an eternal symbol of resistance against tyranny, sacrifice for justice, and love ('isyq) for the Prophet's family (Ahlul Bait). As analyzed by Mahmoud Ayoub, Ashura for the Shia is a rite of "redemptive suffering," where tears and mourning processions not only commemorate the past but also constitute a spiritual participation in Husayn's suffering to gain his intercession.12

CHAPTER IV

Horizons of Interpretation – A Hermeneutical Study on the Construction of Ashura's Meaning

Ashura, as a source-event, is not a dead or monolithic text; it is an open canvas upon which each community paints its existential understanding. The seemingly fundamental differences between the Sunni celebration, the Shia mourning, and the syncretic Nusantara rituals are not merely theological variations, but representations of three distinct "text-worlds" born from different horizons of understanding. Using the analytical tool of hermeneutics, we can deconstruct how each community does not passively receive meaning but actively constructs it as an answer to their most fundamental existential questions, historical needs, and spiritual longings.

1. The Hermeneutics of Conservation and Continuity: The Sunni Reading
The Sunni reading of the Ashura text is a stabilizing hermeneutic. Its horizon of understanding was shaped by the post-fitna (civil war) need to affirm order and the continuity of prophetic authority. Within this horizon, the community approaches the Ashura text with a pre-understanding (Vorverständnis) that the most authentic meaning is that which is directly connected to the Prophet. Consequently, the narrative of Moses's victory is privileged, while the narrative of the Karbala tragedy is marginalized from the realm of public ritual. This is a "politics of memory" that chooses to activate a unifying memory. This reading, in essence, "freezes" the meaning of Ashura at its idealized moment in Medina to protect the community from its own historical trauma.

2. The Hermeneutics of Resistance and Presence: The Shia Reading
If the Sunni reading is about continuity, the Shia reading is a hermeneutic of rupture and hope. It is born from the horizon of a community that experienced marginalization. They approach the Ashura text with a "hermeneutics of suspicion" toward the official narrative. Their interpretive act is radical: they elevate the event of Karbala into a transhistorical archetype. Karbala no longer "happened" in 680 CE; it "happens" at all times. Here, the absence of a saving miracle is read as the highest sign of God's presence. The narrative identity of this community, as analyzed by Paul Ricoeur, is constructed through the repetition of this story of suffering, transforming trauma into a source of strength.13

3. The Syncretic and Cosmic Hermeneutics: The Nusantara Reading
The Nusantara reading, particularly in Java, displays a third hermeneutical approach that rejects dichotomy. Its horizon of understanding is agrarian and cosmic, where the ultimate goals are harmony (rukun) and well-being (keselamatan). Its interpretive act is synthesis: it does not choose, but embraces. The Bubur Suro (Suro Porridge) ritual is a symbolic text of this reading, which dissolves various stories into a single, larger cosmic narrative, prioritizing socio-communal function over its historical-theological precision.14

CHAPTER V

Behind the Veil of Events – The Essence of Ashura in Sufistic Contemplation

In the view of the inner eye (ayn al-qalb), the 10th of Muharram is not merely a date on the calendar but a gateway to a deeper spiritual reality. From a Sufistic perspective, exoteric history (zahir) is a veil behind which lies an eternal essence (haqiqah) relevant to the journey of every soul (suluk). Ashura, in its essence, is a cosmic drama about the human soul on its journey to God, presenting three great curricula in the spiritual school.

1. The Path of Moses (Thariq al-Musawi): The Curriculum of Liberation and Majesty (Jalal) In an esoteric reading, the story of the Israelites' liberation is an archetype for the liberation of every soul from the tyranny of the ego (Nafs al-Ammarah). The miracle of the parting sea is a manifestation of Tajalli al-Jalal, the Self-disclosure of God's Majesty. The Sufistic lesson is the acknowledgment of one's own powerlessness (faqr) and the realization that the only true savior is God (Al-Munji), which is responded to with gratitude for liberation from the prison of the self.

2. The Path of Husayn (Thariq al-Husaini): The Curriculum of Love and Beauty (Jamal) If the Path of Moses is the foundational curriculum, the Path of Husayn is the advanced curriculum on union. The absence of an exoteric intervention at Karbala is a sign of God's trust. Here, theophany no longer occurs through natural events but within the sacred person himself. Husayn becomes the Al-Insan al-Kamil (the Perfect Human), a clear mirror (mazhar) for God's Attributes. Karbala is an epic of 'isyq (divine love), and his martyrdom is the pinnacle of fana' fi-Llah—the annihilation of the lover in the Beloved. The learning here is paradoxical: true life is found in death, and the deepest inner knowledge (ma'rifah) is born from the womb of suffering.

3. The Javanese Cosmic Path (Lelaku Suro): The Curriculum of Harmony and Perfection (Kamal) The Javanese Sufistic path offers a unique horizontal and cosmic contemplation. For a wong laku (a Javanese spiritual journeyman), the month of Suro is not primarily about historical events but about a fundamental shift in the universe's energy. It is a sacred wanci (time) when the veil separating the physical world (alam lair) from the unseen world (alam batin) becomes dramatically thin. This cosmic shift is understood through rasa—a sharp spiritual intuition—as a moment fraught with power yet also full of danger. Consequently, the way of understanding and responding to this sacred moment gives rise to two different yet complementary modes of spirituality: the active, elite path of the court (kraton) and the defensive, communal path of the agrarians.

The Kraton-Centric Perspective: The Path of Uniting with Cosmic Power Within the circles of nobles, poets, and court intellectuals, as reflected in the serat (classical manuscripts), the month of Suro is a golden opportunity to absorb abundant cosmic energy. For them, the King is not merely a political leader but a microcosmic representation of God on earth, a khalifatullah responsible for the balance of the macrocosm. Therefore, the laku prihatin (ascetic practices) performed during Suro—such as tapa brata (austerities) and tirakat (meditative retreats) in sacred places—are not just for personal piety. Their purpose is politico-metaphysical: to strengthen the wahyu keprabon (divine mandate to rule) and to absorb the kasekten (spiritual power) needed to maintain the stability of the kingdom and the universe.

This grand philosophy is perfectly manifested in the meaning of the titles of the Mataram rulers, which are not just names but an ontological manifesto of their spiritual roles: Mangkubumi (“He Who Holds the Earth.” This is the fundamental role as the preserver of the earth and the welfare of its people); Paku Buwono (“The Nail/Axis of the World.” This is an elevation of the role to a cosmic stabilizer who keeps the world from disintegration); and Hamengkubuwono (“He Who Holds the Cosmos in His Lap.” This is the pinnacle of synthesis, where the king must be able to embrace the entire universe in the lap of his wisdom). The laku during Suro is a deep meditation to achieve the realization of Manunggaling Kawula Gusti, uniting the microcosm (the king's self) with the macrocosm (the universe).15

The Agrarian Perspective Beyond the Kraton: The Path of Harmony with the Unseen World For the rural communities, whose lives depend directly on the rhythm of nature, the understanding of Suro is more communal and defensive. The month is considered angker (eerie) and wingit (sacredly potent). The thinning of the veil between the two worlds means that the lelembut (subtle spirits) and unseen natural forces become more active. If the court elites actively seek power, the common people are more focused on not disturbing the fragile balance. The main focus is to maintain keselamatan (safety) and kerukunan (social harmony) in the village. This is expressed through social prohibitions or ora ilok (taboos against holding celebrations). The primary rituals are communal and preventative: the slametan (communal feast) and the making of Bubur Suro. The purpose of these rituals is tolak bala (to ward off disaster) and ngalap berkah (to seek blessings) from all inhabitants of the cosmos: from God Almighty, the prophets, the rice goddess (Dewi Sri), the saints (wali), ancestral spirits, and the guardian spirits (danyang) of the village.16

These two perspectives, though different, are born from the same worldview: a profound awareness of a non-physical reality that influences life. They reflect the third manifestation of God's Attributes: Tajalli al-Kamal, the Self-disclosure of Perfection, which is the harmonious balance between Majesty (Jalal) and Beauty (Jamal), pursued both through the self-conquest of the knights and the communal prayers of the peasants on their eternal journey to understand sangkan paraning dumadi—from whence we came and to where we shall return.

EPILOGUE

AN UNENDING DIALOGUE

The journey of the 10th of Muharram from a pre-Islamic Arab tribal celebration to a marker of such diverse identities is extraordinary proof of the fluidity of meaning within religious tradition. It demonstrates that no ritual has a single or static meaning. As a sacred palimpsest, Ashura has absorbed and reflected narratives of gratitude, atonement, human tragedy, and political resistance.

Historically, it is rooted in the universal symbolism of the "tenth day." Linguistically, it is a heritage of the Semitic world. Theologically, it is both a meeting point and a breaking point in the great Abrahamic family. And through interpretation, it becomes a mirror in which each community can see a reflection of its deepest identity, hopes, and sufferings. In the end, Ashura affirms that history is not only shaped by events, but by the way we choose to remember, interpret, and witness the traces (athar) of God behind them.

GLOSSARY

Agonalia (Ancient Roman)
One of the ancient Roman festivals held in January, dedicated to the god Janus, involving sacrificial rituals.
Ahlul Bait (Arabic: أهل البيت)
Literally "People of the House." Refers to the family of the Prophet Muhammad, with a central spiritual meaning and status in Shia theology.
Alam lair / alam batin (Javanese)
A concept of duality in Javanese cosmology that distinguishes between the "outer/physical world" and the "inner/unseen world."
Angker / Wingit (Javanese)
A quality attributed to a place or time that is considered sacred, magically potent, and potentially dangerous if not respected.
Archetype (Concept)
An original model, pattern, or universal symbol. In the essay, Karbala is referred to as an archetype of the struggle between truth and tyranny.
Athar (Arabic: أثر)
"Trace" or "imprint." Used in a metaphysical context to refer to the traces of God's manifestations in the universe.
Ayn al-qalb (Sufi: عين القلب)
"The eye of the heart." A mystical term for the faculty of inner perception or spiritual intuition capable of "seeing" the essential reality behind exoteric forms.
Azadari (Persian/Urdu: عزاداری)
A term referring to the series of mourning ceremonies commemorating the martyrdom of Imam Husayn.
Batin (Arabic: باطن)
The internal, hidden, or esoteric aspect of a thing, as opposed to its external (zahir) aspect.
'Isyq (Arabic: عشق)
A term for a profound, ardent, and passionate love, often used to describe divine love.
Fana' fi-Llah (Sufi: فناء في الله)
"Annihilation in God." The peak mystical experience where the consciousness of the personal ego dissolves into the consciousness of the Divine Presence.
Hamengkubuwono (Javanese)
A royal title of the Sultan of Yogyakarta, meaning "He Who Holds the Cosmos in His Lap," reflecting the role of a leader who unifies the entire universe.
Insan Kamil (Sufi: الإنسان الكامل)
"The Perfect Human." A mystical concept of an individual who has achieved spiritual perfection and has become a mirror for God's Attributes.
Jalal (Sufi: جلال)
"Majesty." One of the two main polarities of God's Attributes, encompassing aspects of power, might, and justice.
Jamal (Sufi: جمال)
"Beauty." The other polarity of God's Attributes, encompassing aspects of mercy, gentleness, and intimacy.
Kamal (Sufi: كمال)
"Perfection." An Attribute of God that represents the synthesis and harmonious balance between the aspects of Majesty (Jalal) and Beauty (Jamal).
Kasekten (Javanese)
Spiritual power or invulnerability believed to be attainable through ascetic practices and meditation.
Lelaku Suro (Javanese)
Spiritual or ascetic practices undertaken specifically during the month of Suro (Muharram).
Ma'rifah (Sufi: معرفة)
Direct, intuitive knowledge of God achieved through spiritual experience; gnosis.
Mangkubumi (Javanese)
A high noble title in Mataram meaning "He Who Holds the Earth," implying an active role as the guardian of the world's welfare.
Manunggaling Kawula Gusti (Javanese)
The ultimate concept in Javanese mysticism, meaning "the union of the servant with the Lord."
Mazhar (Sufi: مظهر)
Literally "a place of manifestation." Refers to a person or event that serves as a locus of manifestation or a mirror for God's Attributes.
Nafs al-Ammarah (Sufi: نفس الأمارة)
The soul or ego at its lowest level, which inclines humans toward evil; the "commanding self."
Ngalap Berkah (Javanese)
To seek or request blessings, grace, and positive energy, usually from a sacred place or holy figure.
Ora Ilok (Javanese)
A phrase meaning "it is not proper" or "taboo." Refers to a set of customary prohibitions.
Paku Buwono (Javanese)
A royal title of the Susuhunan of Surakarta, meaning "The Nail/Axis of the World," positioning the king as the axis mundi who maintains cosmic stability.
Palimpsest (Concept)
An ancient manuscript that has been erased to be reused but where traces of the original writing are still faintly visible. Used as a metaphor for the layers of meaning in Ashura.
Politics of Memory (Concept)
The conscious act by a community to select and emphasize certain historical memories to shape a collective identity.
Redemptive Suffering (Theological Terminology)
The idea that suffering undergone by a holy figure has spiritual value to atone for or redeem their community.
Rukun (Javanese)
A state of social harmony, accord, and freedom from conflict. One of the highest values in Javanese societal ethics.
Serat (Javanese)
Literary works or ancient manuscripts in the Javanese tradition, typically containing philosophical or mystical teachings.
Suluk (Sufi: سلوك)
The journey or path-faring of a disciple (salik) to draw closer to God.
Suro (Javanese)
The term in the Javanese calendar and culture for the month of Muharram.
Tajalli (Sufi: تجلّي)
"Self-disclosure," "unveiling," or "manifestation" of God in the universe.
Tolak Bala (Javanese)
A ritual or act intended to repel or prevent the coming of disasters, plagues, or misfortune.
Vorverständnis (Hermeneutics)
A German term from Hans-Georg Gadamer meaning "pre-understanding." It refers to the inevitable assumptions and prejudices an interpreter brings when approaching a text.
Wahyu Keprabon (Javanese)
The divine legitimacy or mandate to rule, believed to descend upon chosen kings in Java.
Yom Kippur (Hebrew)
"Day of Atonement." The holiest day in the Jewish calendar, observed with fasting and prayer.
