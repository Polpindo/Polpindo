Womb on the Altar of Capital: A Requiem for Suminten

By SIGIT N. MASHARY Monday, June 23, 2025 22:09 WIB
REFLECTION FAMILY SOCIAL SPIRITUALITY

In the whispered consciousness of our ancestors' archaic universe, the womb was a sacred haramain—a twin holy space alongside the Ka'bah, where divine mysteries resided. There, sirrullah, the grand secret of creation, was entrusted with reverence.1 Yet in this era, for Suminten, a 28-year-old woman from Bantul's coast, her womb has become a scale of calculation. "Four hundred million," she whispers to herself, as if it were a liberation mantra. "Enough to build a house from a Singaporean's seed." In a Jakarta clinic, the organ once revered as a mihrab—the qibla of life—has metamorphosed into a printing press, a paradoxical anomaly at the heart of a civilization claiming modernity.2

From the depths of time's corridors, the echo of Friedrich Nietzsche's prophecy seems to manifest: God is dead, and now, in sterile rooms smelling of disinfectant, humanity stitches together its biological destiny anew.3 Suminten's gaze, simultaneously vacant and piercing, mirrors the Übermensch rebelling against obsolete morality. Here, the womb is no longer 'aurah mustahazana—a sacred, guarded intimacy—but has become estranged, transformed into the commodity fetish Marx described: a reproductive organ severed from its owner's soul, alienated.4 "This is reproductive autonomy, Ma'am! The right to bodily self-determination," the doctor declares as Suminten presses the hormonal needle into her abdomen once more. Suminten can only murmur bitterly, "It feels more like a field force-fertilized for a bumper harvest." Cognitive psychology names this invisible wound: self-dehumanization, a psychological defense mechanism when the body is reduced to a mere production object.5

"They sell narratives of heaven, but build production hell within."

The "Bunda Sejahtera" Clinic is modernity's temple for an ambiguous age. The sharp scent of antiseptic replaces incense and benzoin. On its waiting room table lies an open Quran at Surah Maryam—a bitter irony about sacred pregnancy now reinterpreted through petri dishes and incubators.6 "We only help," the director says with a polished smile, a perfect example of cognitive dissonance—the ability to frame exploitation as an act of compassion.7 At the technocracy's altar they call an operating room, Suminten's eggs are harvested like pearls from oyster shells. In the silent laboratory next door, these cells meet the client's seed in the quiet of petri dishes. Days later, the chosen embryo is replanted in her womb—a cold, mechanical procedure feeling more like planting hybrid seeds than the dawn of life.

•••

Classical theologians might frown, silently questioning: Where lies the barakah when conception no longer springs from love or mawaddah, but laboratory precision?8 One night, during a devotional gathering, Suminten's phone vibrates softly. A message from an agent: "Client from Qatar. Seeking donor with Javanese bloodline and golden-brown skin. Rp 75 million bonus." This is biocapitalism's naked face: genetics traded like commodities on a black market.9 Faye Ginsburg's theory of stratified reproduction frames this atrocity with academic precision: wombs of women from the Global South have become biological factories for Northern masters.10 Beneath her pristine prayer veil, Suminten hides the folds of a surrogacy contract—a legal artifact symbolizing the eternal struggle between takwa and profit. In Lacanian psychoanalysis, this is the portrait of the split subject: a self divided between identity as God's servant and role as biological vendor.11

As the delivery date approaches, Suminten is confined to the clinic's dormitory. Its walls display absurd slogans like "One Healthy Embryo, One World Hope." Yet behind this humanitarian facade lies an iron law crueler than any jungle. The principle of pacta sunt servanda (agreements bind parties) is unevenly applied. Her contract details Rp 5 million fines for "negligence-induced" miscarriages, prohibitions against seeing her own children, and mandatory haircuts for "sanitary efficiency."12 "They sell heavenly narratives," Suminten grumbles, "but build production hell inside." Here, Giorgio Agamben's bare life theory finds its starkest manifestation: the female body reduced to pure biological machinery, stripped of civil rights, becoming modern homo sacer.13 Java's noble philosophy, sepi ing pamrih, rame ing gawe (selfless in motive, diligent in work), is inverted into tragic parody: profit without selflessness—transactions without spiritual quiet.14

In the realm of ideas, Descartes might shudder. His adage, cogito ergo sum (I think, therefore I am), has been replaced by this clinic's new creed: produco ergo sum—I produce, therefore I am.15 Every fetal heartbeat is recorded and analyzed like stock movements. When the baby is born—healthy, perfect, custom-made—Suminten isn't permitted to touch it. "The baby goes straight to Singapore Airlines cargo," a nurse states flatly. Developmental psychology names this organic separation trauma, a wound deeper and less visible than the C-section scar on her abdomen.16 In Sufi terminology, Suminten has become a khadim—a servant—to a life she births but will never possess.17 The concept of fana', the dissolution of self in Sufism, morphs into a dark parody: not unity with the Divine, but disappearance into global supply chains.18

With four hundred million in her account, Suminten returns to Bantul. Her womb feels like an empty space, a house forcibly rented then its true owner evicted—a poetic description of Marx's alienation.19 She uses the money to build a small shop. Yet whenever she sees a neighbor's baby, tears erupt uncontrollably. "I'm not a mother. I'm not a worker. What am I then?" The question echoes in her soul, manifesting anomie and ontological insecurity—a tremor in existence's foundation when body and essence are commercialized.20 At the prayer gathering, the preacher still extols the womb's nobility. Suminten laughs inwardly, a bitter laugh only she understands. "They sell heaven from the pulpit, but I just sold the only earth I possessed within my body."

•••

The uterine revolution may be the 21st century's grandest illusion. It promises liberation from biological prisons through economic opportunity, only to trap women in new, more sophisticated cages. Major religions condemn it; capitalism praises it as innovation. For Suminten, her womb is now a hollow space haunted by two voices: between sacred verses and cash register chimes. As philosopher Donna Haraway predicted, "We are all cyborgs now—hybrids of flesh and market mechanisms."21 From a Sufi perspective, this may be the peak of ghaflah: supreme spiritual negligence, when humanity begins worshipping the Market it created.22 On one hand, they prostrate to God in mosques. On the other, they worship the Market in laboratories. "I," Suminten tells the night wind at Parangtritis beach, "am caught between two altars: one promising heaven, the other having bought my womb."

Glossary

Alienation (Philosophy/Sociology)
A Marxist term referring to the estrangement of individuals from their labor, its products, themselves, and society as a consequence of capitalist production systems.
Anomie (Sociology)
A condition where social norms weaken or disappear, causing individuals to feel directionless, purposeless, and disconnected from society.
'Aurah Mustahazana (Religious/Sufism)
An Arabic term meaning sacred, guarded, and honored intimacy (parts of the body to be covered).
Barakah (Religious)
Divine blessing or grace believed to bring growth, benefit, and happiness.
Bare Life (Political Philosophy)
Giorgio Agamben's concept referring to human life reduced to mere biological existence, stripped of all political and civil rights.
Biocapitalism (Sociology/Economics)
An advanced form of capitalism where biological life itself—such as genes, cells, and organs—becomes a commodity exploited for economic gain.
Cogito Ergo Sum (Philosophy)
"I think, therefore I am." René Descartes' fundamental proposition forming the basis of Western rationalism.
Cyborg (Philosophy/Technology)
Donna Haraway's concept describing hybrid entities combining biological organisms and machines (or market mechanisms).
Cognitive Dissonance (Psychology)
The mental discomfort experienced when holding two conflicting beliefs, ideas, or values simultaneously.
Fana' (Sufism)
A Sufi concept meaning the "dissolution" or "passing away" of individual self-consciousness to unite with divine consciousness.
Commodity Fetishism (Philosophy/Sociology)
A Marxist concept where commodities (goods/services) in capitalist systems are perceived as having mystical power or intrinsic value, obscuring that their value originates from human labor.
Ghaflah (Sufism)
Spiritual negligence or forgetfulness of God and existence's essence, often due to worldly distractions.
Haramain (Religious)
Literally "two sacred sanctuaries," referring to Mecca and Medina. Metaphorically describes the womb's sanctity in this essay.
Homo Sacer (Philosophy/Roman Law)
A figure in ancient Roman law who could be killed by anyone without it being considered murder, but could not be sacrificed in religious rituals. Agamben uses it to describe individuals outside legal protection.
Khadim (Religious/Social)
An Arabic term meaning "servant" or "attendant."
Surrogacy Contract (Law/Ethics)
A legal agreement between a woman (surrogate mother) who agrees to carry and deliver a child for another party (intended parents).
Mawaddah (Religious)
Sincere love, affection, and tenderness, particularly in an Islamic marital context.
Mihrab (Religious)
A niche in a mosque wall indicating the qibla (Ka'bah direction) for prayer. Used as a metaphor for a spiritual orientation point.
Ontological Insecurity (Psychology/Philosophy)
Profound anxiety or instability concerning the nature of existence, reality, and self-identity.
Pacta Sunt Servanda (Law)
A fundamental legal principle stating that agreements are binding and must be honored by contracting parties.
Produco Ergo Sum (Neologism/Philosophy)
"I produce, therefore I am." A parody of Descartes' adage critiquing modern society's measurement of existence by economic productivity.
Rahmatan lil 'Alamin (Religious)
A Quranic phrase meaning "(sent as) mercy to all the worlds."
Requiem (Music/Literature)
Literally, a Requiem Mass in Catholic tradition offered for departed souls. In arts, refers to a work (music or literature) serving as an elegy or solemn lament for something lost or someone deceased.
Self-Dehumanization (Psychology)
The process where individuals begin perceiving themselves as objects or less than human, often in response to demeaning treatment or exploitative situations.
Sepi Ing Pamrih, Rame Ing Gawe (Javanese Philosophy)
A Javanese life principle meaning "diligent in work without expecting personal reward."
Sirrullah (Sufism)
"God's Secret." A mystical concept referring to hidden divine knowledge or mystery.
Split Subject (Psychoanalysis)
A Lacanian concept stating that the "subject" or "self" is never whole but always divided between consciousness and the unconscious.
Stratified Reproduction (Anthropology/Sociology)
A theory explaining how power (social, economic, racial) shapes reproduction unequally, empowering some groups to reproduce while degrading or exploiting others.
Takwa (Religious)
Consciousness and profound awareness of God's presence, motivating adherence to divine commands and avoidance of prohibitions.
Übermensch (Philosophy)
"Overman" or "Superman." Friedrich Nietzsche's concept of individuals who overcome traditional morality to create their own values.
